About QKD Satellite System

×

This web simulator models Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) over a free-space optical channel between a satellite transmitter and two ground communication terminals (Alice and Bob).

By generating pairs of polarization-entangled photons, the satellite allows Alice and Bob to build a shared, random secret key. Any attempt at wiretapping by an adversary (Eve) perturbs the quantum states, causing measurable bit errors (QBER) that trigger alerts, aborting the key exchange if the eavesdropping exceeds safety limits.

BB84 Protocol Workflow

  1. State Generation: Alice (or the satellite) creates single photons randomized in one of four polarization angles: 0° (Horizontal), 90° (Vertical), 45° (Diagonal), and 135° (Anti-Diagonal). These represent bits 0 or 1 in two different bases (Rectilinear ✛ or Diagonal ✕).
  2. Transmission: The photons are sent over the quantum channel to Bob.
  3. Measurement: Bob measures each photon by randomly choosing either the Rectilinear ✛ or Diagonal ✕ basis. If his basis matches Alice's, he will measure the correct bit value. If it doesn't match, he gets a completely random result.
  4. Sifting: Over a public classical channel, Alice and Bob announce which basis they used for each photon, but NOT the bit value. They discard bits where their bases mismatched, leaving the Raw Shifted Key.
  5. Error Rate Estimation: They compare a small public subset of their key to measure the Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER). An eavesdropper measuring the photons changes their quantum state (No-Cloning Theorem), driving the error rate up from ~5% (normal channel noise) to over 11%.
  6. Error Correction & Privacy Amplification: If the error rate is safe (<10%), they run error correction codes (e.g. Cascade) and compress the key using universal hashing (Privacy Amplification) to strip any partial knowledge Eve might have gathered, producing the final secure key.
